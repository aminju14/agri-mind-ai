Banana (Musa spp.) is a fast-growing tropical herb, not a tree, producing fruit 9–12 months
after planting. It needs deep, well-drained, fertile soil, high humidity, warmth (26–30°C),
and protection from strong wind, which shreds leaves and topples plants.

Plant healthy tissue-culture plantlets or disease-free suckers at 2–3 m spacing. Bananas are
very heavy feeders of potassium and nitrogen; a mature mat can remove large amounts of K per
year. Apply fertilizer in frequent small doses and maintain a thick organic mulch to conserve
moisture and feed the soil.

Water is essential — bananas have shallow roots and suffer quickly from drought, but also rot
in waterlogged soil, so drainage matters. Remove excess suckers, keeping one "follower" per
mat so the planting renews itself. De-leaf old and diseased leaves to improve airflow and cut
disease pressure.

Major threats are Panama disease (Fusarium wilt), black Sigatoka leaf streak, and banana
weevil. Once Fusarium infects soil it persists for years, so use resistant varieties and
clean planting material. Harvest the bunch when fingers are full and rounded but still green,
then ripen off the plant.
