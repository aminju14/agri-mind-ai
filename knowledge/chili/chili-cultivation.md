Chili (Capsicum annuum / frutescens) is a warm-season crop that loves heat (24–30°C), full
sun, and well-drained soil rich in organic matter with a pH of 5.5–6.8. It is sensitive to
waterlogging, which quickly causes root rot.

Raise seedlings in trays for 25–35 days, then transplant at 50–60 cm between plants and
60–70 cm between rows. Mulch with straw or plastic to conserve moisture, suppress weeds, and
keep soil from splashing disease spores onto leaves.

Chili needs steady moisture but hates wet feet — use drip irrigation or water at the base,
never overhead, to keep foliage dry and reduce fungal disease. Water stress during flowering
and fruiting causes flower drop and small fruit. Feed with a balanced fertilizer early, then
shift toward more potassium during fruiting to improve fruit set and color.

Common problems include anthracnose (sunken dark fruit lesions), bacterial wilt, aphids, and
thrips. Pinch the first few flowers on young plants to build a stronger frame before fruiting.
Harvest fruit at the green or red stage depending on market; frequent picking encourages more
fruit. Drying chili is a good hedge against price swings and post-harvest loss.
