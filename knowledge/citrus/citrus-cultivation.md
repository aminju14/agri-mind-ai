Citrus (orange, lime, lemon, mandarin) are evergreen subtropical trees needing full sun,
well-drained slightly acidic soil (pH 6.0–6.5), and protection from frost and waterlogging.
Most grafted citrus begin bearing in 2–4 years and can produce for decades.

Plant on a slight mound to improve drainage; citrus roots are very sensitive to standing
water, which invites root rot (Phytophthora). Keep the trunk base dry and mulch away from the
trunk. Water deeply and regularly, especially during flowering and fruit set, but let the top
few centimeters dry between waterings.

Citrus are sensitive to micronutrient deficiencies. Interveinal yellowing on young leaves
signals iron or zinc deficiency, often from high soil pH; uniform pale older leaves point to
nitrogen. Feed little and often with a citrus-specific fertilizer containing micronutrients.

The most serious threat is Huanglongbing (citrus greening), spread by the Asian citrus psyllid,
which causes blotchy mottled leaves and small, lopsided, bitter fruit; there is no cure, so
control the psyllid and use clean nursery stock. Harvest by color and taste — citrus does not
sweeten further after picking.
