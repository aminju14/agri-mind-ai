Corn (Zea mays) is a warm-season cereal that needs full sun, well-drained soil, and steady
moisture. It grows best at 21–30°C and is sensitive to both drought and waterlogging. The
crop matures in 90–120 days depending on the variety.

Plant seeds 3–5 cm deep at 20–25 cm within rows and 70–75 cm between rows, aiming for about
66,000–75,000 plants per hectare. Corn is wind-pollinated, so plant in blocks of several
short rows rather than one long single row to ensure good kernel set.

Corn is a heavy feeder, especially of nitrogen. Apply about one third of nitrogen at
planting and the rest in 1–2 side-dressings at the V6 (six-leaf) and V10 stages. Potassium
and phosphorus go in at planting. Yellowing of lower leaves in a V-shape from the tip is the
classic sign of nitrogen deficiency.

Water is most critical from tasseling through grain fill; drought at silking causes poor
kernel set and big yield losses. Keep the root zone moist but never waterlogged. Control
weeds early — corn competes poorly in the first 4–6 weeks. Harvest for grain when kernels
are hard and the husks are dry, at about 20–25% grain moisture, then dry to 14% for storage.
