Most crop diseases are caused by fungi, bacteria, or viruses, and correct diagnosis starts
with reading the symptom pattern. Where symptoms begin (old vs. young leaves), their shape,
and the weather all narrow the cause.

Early blight (Alternaria solani) on tomato and potato shows as brown spots with concentric
rings ("target spots") and a yellow halo, starting on the lower, older leaves. It thrives in
warm, humid conditions. Remove affected lower leaves, water at the base, mulch to stop soil
splash, and apply a copper-based or chlorothalonil fungicide if it spreads — always read the
label and follow local regulations.

Distinguishing disease from nutrient deficiency is key: target-like brown rings with a yellow
halo mean fungal blight, whereas nutrient yellowing is uniform and lacks rings. Bacterial
diseases often cause water-soaked spots, ooze, and rapid wilting. Viral diseases cause mosaic
mottling, leaf curling, and stunting and are usually spread by insects — there is no cure, so
remove infected plants and control the vector.

General principles: a text-only or photo diagnosis is a likely cause, not a certainty —
confirm with a local plant clinic. Always start with sanitation and cultural controls (remove
infected tissue, improve airflow, avoid overhead watering) before reaching for chemicals.
