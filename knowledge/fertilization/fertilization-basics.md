title: Fertilization Strategy and Soil Nutrition

Plants need three macronutrients in large amounts: nitrogen (N) for leaves and vegetative
growth, phosphorus (P) for roots and flowering, and potassium (K) for fruit quality, water
regulation, and stress tolerance. Secondary nutrients (calcium, magnesium, sulfur) and
micronutrients (iron, zinc, boron) are needed in smaller amounts but cause real problems when
short.

Always start with a soil test. It reveals pH and nutrient levels so you fertilize what the
soil actually lacks instead of guessing. Soil pH controls nutrient availability: most crops do
best at pH 6.0–6.8. Strongly acidic soils lock up phosphorus and benefit from lime; alkaline
soils cause iron and zinc deficiency.

Split nitrogen into several applications across the season rather than one big dose — N leaches
easily, especially in sandy soils and after heavy rain, and a single dose is wasteful and can
burn roots. Phosphorus and potassium are usually applied as a basal dose at planting. Organic
matter (compost, manure) improves soil structure, water-holding, and slow-release nutrition;
combine it with mineral fertilizer for the best results.

Reading deficiencies: uniform yellowing of older leaves usually means nitrogen; purpling can
mean phosphorus; yellow leaf margins and weak stalks suggest potassium; interveinal yellowing
of young leaves points to iron or zinc, often a pH problem rather than a true shortage.
