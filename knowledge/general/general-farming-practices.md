title: General Farming Practices for Smallholders

Successful smallholder farming starts with the soil, not the seed. Before planting, run a quick
soil texture and pH check — it tells you what will actually thrive instead of what you hope will.
Most first-season losses come from skipping the soil test, not from inexperience.

Start small and learn the full grow-to-harvest loop on two or three high-value, fast-cycle crops
before scaling. Lay out beds around sunlight and drainage, not convenience. Keep a simple log of
what you plant and when — records turn each season into data you can improve on.

Crop rotation breaks pest and disease cycles and balances soil nutrients: follow a heavy feeder
(like corn) with a legume (like beans) that fixes nitrogen, and avoid planting the same family in
the same spot year after year. Mulching conserves moisture, suppresses weeds, moderates soil
temperature, and feeds soil life as it breaks down.

Plan around your real constraints — soil, climate, and water access — rather than trends, and
test new ideas on a small patch before committing the whole field. Soil temperature and rainfall
onset, not the calendar date, are the real signals for when to sow. Good farming decisions are
local: the best general answer is the one you verify on your own land.
