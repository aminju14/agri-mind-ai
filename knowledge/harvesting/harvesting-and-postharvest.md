---
title: Harvesting and Post-Harvest Handling
source: agrimind.ai
category: harvesting
lang: en
---

Harvesting at the right maturity and handling produce carefully afterward protects yield,
quality, and price. Picking too early gives poor flavor and size; too late invites pests,
disease, and shattering. Maturity signs differ by crop: grain hardness and moisture for cereals,
skin color and firmness for fruit, and days-after-planting for vegetables.

Harvest in the cool of early morning when produce is turgid and field heat is low, and handle
gently — bruises become rot. Use clean, ventilated containers and avoid overfilling. For grains
like rice and corn, dry promptly to a safe storage moisture (about 14%) to prevent mold and
aflatoxin; sun-drying on tarps or mechanical dryers both work.

Post-harvest losses in the tropics can reach 20–40% from poor handling, heat, and pests. Cool
the produce quickly, keep it out of direct sun, sort out damaged units that spread rot, and
store in clean, dry, ventilated conditions. Perishables benefit from cold storage; staples need
dry, sealed storage protected from rodents and insects.

Adding value through drying, grading, and proper packaging raises returns and smooths price
swings. For volatile crops like chili, drying converts a perishable glut into a storable product
that can be sold when prices recover.
