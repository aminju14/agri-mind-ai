title: Irrigation Methods and Water Management
source: agrimind.ai

Good irrigation delivers the right amount of water at the right time without waste or
waterlogging. The best method depends on the crop, water supply, soil, and budget. The three
main systems are surface (furrow/flood), sprinkler, and drip.

Drip irrigation delivers water directly to the root zone through emitters, achieving 90%+
efficiency. It keeps foliage dry — which reduces fungal disease — saves water and fertilizer
(via fertigation), and suits row crops like chili, tomato, and vegetables. Its drawbacks are
higher upfront cost and emitter clogging, which a filter and periodic flushing prevent.

Sprinklers suit field crops and uneven land but lose water to evaporation and wet the foliage,
raising disease risk; irrigate in early morning to limit both. Surface irrigation is cheap and
simple but the least efficient and needs level land. Rice uses controlled flooding, but
alternate wetting and drying (AWD) cuts water use sharply without yield loss.

Schedule by the crop's need and the soil, not the calendar. Sandy soils need smaller, more
frequent waterings; clay soils hold water longer. Water deeply to encourage deep roots, and
check soil moisture by feel at root depth before irrigating. The most water-sensitive stages
are flowering and fruit/grain fill — never let the crop stress then.
