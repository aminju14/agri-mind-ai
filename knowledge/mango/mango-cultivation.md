Mango (Mangifera indica) is a deep-rooted tropical tree that fruits seasonally, usually after
a dry, cool period that triggers flowering. It tolerates a range of soils if drainage is good
and prefers a distinct dry season before bloom. Grafted trees begin bearing in 3–5 years.

Plant grafted seedlings at 8–10 m spacing (closer for high-density orchards). Young trees need
regular watering, but mature trees benefit from water stress before flowering — irrigation
during the rest period can push vegetative flushes instead of flowers.

Flowering and fruit set are the critical window. Anthracnose and powdery mildew attack flowers
and young fruit in humid weather, causing heavy fruit drop; protective sprays and good airflow
help. Mango hopper and fruit fly are the main insect pests; bagging fruit and orchard sanitation
reduce fruit fly damage.

Prune after harvest to maintain an open canopy, remove dead wood, and control height. Apply
fertilizer in 2–3 splits, heavier on potassium near fruiting. Harvest mature-green fruit when
shoulders fill out and skin color begins to change; mangoes ripen well off the tree.
