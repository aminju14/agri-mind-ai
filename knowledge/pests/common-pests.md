Insect pests damage crops by sucking sap, chewing tissue, or transmitting viruses. Integrated
Pest Management (IPM) combines monitoring, cultural controls, biological control, and chemicals
only as a last resort, which protects beneficial insects and slows resistance.

Sap-sucking pests include aphids, whiteflies, thrips, and mites. They cluster on shoot tips
and leaf undersides, cause curling and stunting, excrete honeydew that grows sooty mold, and
spread viruses. Strong water sprays, insecticidal soap or neem, and conserving natural enemies
(ladybirds, lacewings, predatory mites) usually keep them in check.

Chewing pests include caterpillars (armyworm, fruit borer) and beetles. Hand-pick where
practical, use pheromone traps to monitor and trap, and apply Bacillus thuringiensis (Bt) for
caterpillars, which spares beneficials. Fruit flies are managed with sanitation (remove fallen
fruit), bagging, and protein-bait traps.

IPM principles: scout fields weekly and act on thresholds, not on the first insect seen.
Rotate crops to break pest cycles, keep fields weed-free to remove alternate hosts, and rotate
chemical classes to avoid resistance. When spraying is needed, choose selective products, spray
in the evening to protect pollinators, and always follow the label and pre-harvest interval.
