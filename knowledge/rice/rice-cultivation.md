Rice (Oryza sativa) is a semi-aquatic cereal grown mainly in flooded paddies. It thrives
in warm climates (20–35°C) with abundant water and full sun. The crop cycle for most
lowland varieties runs 110–150 days from transplanting to harvest.

Land preparation begins with puddling: the field is flooded and tilled to create a soft,
level mud layer that holds water and suppresses weeds. A well-leveled field is critical —
uneven fields waste water and give patchy growth.

Seedlings are raised in a nursery for 18–25 days, then transplanted at 2–3 seedlings per
hill with 20×20 cm spacing. Direct seeding is also possible and saves labor but needs
better weed control.

Water management is the heart of rice farming. Keep 3–5 cm of standing water during the
vegetative stage, then practice alternate wetting and drying (AWD) to save water without
yield loss: let the field dry until the water level drops 15 cm below the surface, then
re-flood. Maintain continuous shallow flooding during flowering, the most water-sensitive
stage.

Nitrogen is applied in 2–3 splits: at basal (transplanting), active tillering, and panicle
initiation. Over-applying nitrogen causes lodging and pest outbreaks. Phosphorus and
potassium are usually applied as basal. Harvest when 80–85% of the grains are straw-colored
and the upper grains are firm; drain the field 7–10 days before harvest.
